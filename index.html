How to use each tab

Play

Type a yardage and get a club call that weighs stock distance, consistency, spin, descent, smash, and your miss pattern. Alternates show trust and curve so you can choose the safer window.

Live

On-course GPS. Tap Start and allow location. It finds your course and hole automatically, shows front, middle, and back of the green, and calls a club. Drop a pin on the satellite map to get distances to the actual hole location instead of the middle. When there is an elevation change it shows a "plays like" number and uses that for the club call.

Bag

Every club with its stock carry. Tap a club for the full stat sheet: carry, total, window, consistency, speeds, smash, apex, spin, miss pattern, and how each number stacks up against PGA, scratch, and 10-handicap benchmarks.

Compare

Each club against PGA Tour, scratch, or 10-handicap benchmarks. Use the stat chips to switch what's being compared: carry, total, ball speed, club speed, smash, spin, or apex. The green bar is you, the yellow mark is the benchmark.

Insights

Ball striking profile, swing DNA (path, face, face to path), flight windows, gapping, consistency, and start/finish miss map. Built from every launch monitor field so strategy matches how you deliver the club.

What each metric means

Carry

How far the ball flies in the air before it lands, in yards. This is the number that matters for clearing bunkers, water, and false fronts.

Total

Carry plus roll, where the ball finishes. Use it for tee shots and running approaches.

Stock carry

Your reliable carry for a club, with your best and worst shot trimmed off so one flush strike or one chunk doesn't skew it. Club calls are based on this number.

Window

Your shortest and longest carry with that club. If trouble sits inside the window, the club can find it.

Consistency (0 to 100)

How repeatable your distance is with that club. Higher is tighter. Below about 70, expect a wider spread and favor the safe miss.

Smash factor

Ball speed divided by club speed, a measure of strike quality. Driver around 1.45+ is efficient. A low number means off center contact, not lack of speed.

Spin (rpm)

Backspin rate. Wedges spin high (9k to 10k) and stop fast. Driver spins low (2k to 3k) and runs out. Unusually low spin on an iron usually means a flyer that goes long.

Spin axis / miss bias

Which way your shots curve on average. Negative or "L" means draw/left, positive or "R" means fade/right. Aim to let your typical curve work toward the target.

Apex

Peak height of the shot in feet. Higher apex lands steeper and stops faster. Lower flight runs out more and handles wind better.

Launch / descent / horiz

Launch is the starting angle of the ball. Descent is how steep it comes down. Horiz is left or right launch direction off the target line. Higher launch and steeper descent help irons and wedges stop.

Face, path, face to path

Club path is the direction the head is moving. Face angle is where the face points at impact. Face to path is the difference that creates curve. Positive path is out to in for a right handed player (fade bias). Negative face to path means the face is closed to the path (draw spin).

Side carry / side total

How far left or right of the target line the ball is at landing (side carry) and after it stops (side total). Measured on the launch monitor. This is your real dispersion, not an estimate from spin axis.

Offline / spread

Offline is average yards left or right of the target line. Spread is the width from your furthest left miss to furthest right, the dispersion corridor you need to leave yourself.

Gapping

The yardage difference between neighboring clubs. Ideal is 10 to 15 yards. A big gap leaves in between distances. A small gap means two clubs do the same job.

Plays like

GPS distance adjusted for elevation, using roughly one yard per three feet of change. Uphill adds, downhill subtracts. It does not account for wind or temperature.

F / M / B

Distance to the front, middle, and back of the green. Front tells you what you must carry, back tells you what is too much.
